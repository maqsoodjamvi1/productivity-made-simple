Parkinson’s Law states that work expands to fill the time available for its completion. If you give yourself a week to write a report, it will take you a week. If you give yourself two hours, you will find a way to finish it in two hours.

I spent years falling into the trap of “planning for the worst.” I would block out entire afternoons for simple tasks, thinking I was being realistic. Instead, those tasks inevitably bled into my entire day, leaving me drained and feeling like I hadn’t actually accomplished anything of value. Once I started shrinking my deadlines, my output didn’t just increase—my stress levels plummeted.

Here is how you can use this principle to stop procrastinating and actually reclaim your time.

Identify the “Efficiency Threshold”

The biggest mistake most people make is setting a deadline based on how long a task could take if they were distracted or working at a slow pace. Instead, you need to identify your “efficiency threshold”—the minimum amount of time required to do the task well, assuming you are focused.

To find this, look at a recurring task. Let’s say you spend every Friday morning answering emails and preparing weekly updates. You’ve allotted three hours for this. Stop and ask yourself: If I were forced to leave for an emergency in 45 minutes, could I get the essential parts of this done?

Usually, the answer is yes. You’d prioritize the urgent replies, skip the fluff, and streamline your reporting. That 45-minute window is your new “Sprint” deadline. By artificially constraining your time, you force your brain to prioritize the high-impact actions and discard the “busy work” that fills the gaps.

The 60-Minute Sprint Method

I rarely set a task duration longer than 60 minutes anymore. If a project is large, I break it into “Sprintable” chunks. Here is how I set up my daily workflow using this method:

  • The Triage: Before starting, spend exactly five minutes listing the specific outcome of the sprint. Don’t write “work on the presentation.” Write “complete slides 1 through 5 with bullet points and placeholders.”
  • The Environment Reset: Clear your desk. Close every browser tab except the one you need. Put your phone in another room. The time constraint only works if you remove the ability to toggle between tasks.
  • The Hard Stop: Set a visible timer. When the timer hits zero, the task is effectively “done.” If you aren’t finished, you evaluate: Did the task actually take longer, or were you distracted? If you were focused and still didn’t finish, you now have a more accurate data point for your next sprint.

Why Shorter Deadlines Kill Procrastination

Procrastination thrives on the feeling that you have “plenty of time.” When you have a week to do something, your brain treats the task as a low-priority background process. You feel guilty about not doing it, but you also feel no urgency to start.

When you shrink a deadline to 45 or 60 minutes, the task becomes an immediate threat to your current comfort. You stop “preparing” to work and start working. You stop looking for the perfect font and start typing the content.

I’ve found that the quality of my work actually increases under these conditions. When I have three hours to write a memo, I overthink the tone, rewrite the opening paragraph ten times, and second-guess my data. When I have 45 minutes, I write the core message clearly, verify the facts, and move on. The “perfectionism” that usually causes procrastination disappears when the clock is ticking.

What to Do When You Hit a Wall

Sometimes, you will misjudge. You might set a 30-minute deadline for a task that genuinely requires an hour of deep focus. This is not a failure; it is a diagnostic tool.

If you hit your deadline and aren’t done, don’t just keep working indefinitely. Stop, take a two-minute break, and reset the timer for the remaining amount of time you need. By breaking the work into timed blocks, you prevent the “drift” where you lose track of time or start checking social media because you feel like you have all day to recover.

Treat your work like an athlete training for a sprint. You aren’t trying to run a marathon every day; you are trying to maximize your speed for short, high-intensity bursts. The result isn’t just getting more done—it is getting your evenings and weekends back because you stopped letting your tasks expand to fill your entire life.

If you are ready to stop letting your to-do list dictate your schedule, start by picking one project you have been putting off. Cut your estimated time for it in half, set a timer, and see how much you can accomplish before the clock runs out. Once you see the results, you can begin applying this to larger, more complex projects.
