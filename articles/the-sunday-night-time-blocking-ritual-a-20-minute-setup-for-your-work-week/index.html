I used to spend my Monday mornings in a state of reactive chaos. I would open my inbox at 8:30 AM, see a barrage of requests, and spend the first two hours of the day putting out fires instead of doing the work that actually mattered. By Tuesday afternoon, I was already behind, and by Friday, I was exhausted.

The shift happened when I started treating Sunday night like a runway check. I stopped using my calendar just for appointments and started using it as a map for my actual labor. It takes me exactly 20 minutes to block out my week, and it is the single most effective habit I have for protecting my sanity.

The 20-Minute Ritual: A Step-by-Step Breakdown

You don’t need a fancy app or a complicated system. I use a standard digital calendar and a blank notepad. The goal isn’t to be perfect; the goal is to be intentional.

Phase 1: The Brain Dump (5 Minutes) Before touching your calendar, write down every single thing you need to accomplish. Don’t organize it yet. Just get it out of your head. If you have a project for a client, a bill to pay, and a doctor’s appointment to schedule, list them all. If it’s on paper, your brain stops spending energy trying to “remember” it.

Phase 2: The “Musts” and the “Shoulds” (5 Minutes) Look at your list. Pick your top three non-negotiable tasks for the week. These are the items that, if completed, would make the week a success regardless of what else happens. Next, look at your calendar and lock in your fixed commitments first—team meetings, recurring calls, or school drop-offs. If your calendar isn’t blocked for these, you’ll accidentally schedule over them.

Phase 3: The Time Blocks (10 Minutes) Now, take your top three “musts” and drop them into the empty slots on your calendar. I assign these to the time of day when I have the most focus. For me, that’s 9:00 AM to 11:00 AM.

If a task looks like it will take four hours, don’t just put one block; break it into two two-hour sessions. If you leave a project as a generic “do this week” item, you will inevitably push it off until Thursday. If it has a specific home on your calendar, it becomes an appointment with yourself that you wouldn’t dream of canceling.

Why This Works (And Where Most People Go Wrong)

The biggest mistake people make with time blocking is assuming their calendar needs to be full. If you block out every single minute of your 40-hour work week, you will fail the moment one email takes five minutes longer than expected.

I follow the 60/40 rule. I only “hard block” 60% of my workday. The other 40% is left as “flex time.” This is where the unexpected stuff happens: a quick Slack message from a colleague, a client who calls with an urgent question, or a task that took longer than anticipated. When the chaos happens, I don’t feel like my system has failed because I intentionally left room for it.

Another secret: Don’t just block “Work.” Be specific. Instead of putting “Project X” on your calendar, use labels like “Drafting Project X report” or “Editing project X slides.” When you look at your calendar on Tuesday morning, you don’t have to spend mental energy deciding what to do. You just execute.

Managing the “Reality Gap”

Inevitably, your week will not go exactly as planned. On Tuesday, a meeting might run over, or you might hit a wall of fatigue. When this happens, don’t abandon the system.

Instead, perform a “micro-reset” at the end of every day. If you didn’t finish your blocked task, move it to the next available slot on Wednesday. Do not let the missed task hover in your mind like a ghost. By moving it to a specific spot, you regain control. You aren’t “behind”; you are simply adjusting your schedule to match reality.

The Sunday night ritual is not about being a robot. It’s about creating a plan so that when Monday morning arrives, you don’t have to waste your morning energy figuring out what to do. You simply open your calendar and start working.
